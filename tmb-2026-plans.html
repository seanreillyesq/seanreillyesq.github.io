Nine days walking round Mont Blanc, eleven nights in a van - and one of them under canvas. Sean and Alex walk the full 179 km loop, bivouacking below the Col des Fours on night two; Nicole leapfrogs the van between valley campsites and shuttles the walkers to and from the trailheads. This page is the whole plan - maps, bookings, phone numbers, and what to do when it goes sideways.

If it all goes wrong. 112 gets mountain rescue in all three countries. France also has PGHM Chamonix direct: +33 4 50 53 16 89. Switzerland: 144, or 1414 for the Rega helicopter. Sean's mobile: +44 7957 247666. Separated with no signal? Don't wander - carry on to that day's van base. It's on every day card below. (Friday 31st is the exception: the walkers' overnight is the Mottets bivouac, and the van is ahead at Val Veny - Saturday's rendezvous.)

The numbers. 179 km on foot over nine days, campsite door to campsite door, measured on the actual trails rather than the guidebook estimates. Longest day is the last, at 28 km; biggest climb is Day 2 over the Col des Fours, carried packs and all. Nicole drives roughly 470 km all in - 330 km of relocations plus the shuttle runs. Anti-clockwise from Les Houches, the classic direction. Arrive Tuesday 28 July, boots on Thursday 30, finish Friday 7 August.

The rules that bite. Bellevue's gate locks at 19:30 and that's that - arrivals 16:00-19:30, passports for all three at check-in, pitches are hard standing (tent pegs won't go in), and there's no water or waste dump on site; the free borne is at the Carrefour Market as you come into Chamonix. Le Pontet wants the €22.18 balance on arrival. The Italian Val Ferret road only lets cars as far as Lavachey - 260 paid spaces, 09:00-19:00, and they close the road when they're gone. Switzerland: buy the CHF 40 vignette before the Grand St Bernard motorway, and do the big shop in Italy first - Swiss supermarket prices are roughly double. Argentière takes no bookings at all; the job there is simply to be at reception at 14:00 when it opens.

Offline maps (GPX / KMZ)

Phone maps are useless on the cols - there's barely a bar of signal up there. Load the day's GPX into OsmAnd, Organic Maps or a Garmin the night before, and keep this page for the phone numbers and pickup plans.

Day by day

Distances and climbs are measured off the mapped route against the EU elevation model, not lifted from a guidebook - and they run campsite door to campsite door, which is why some days look longer than the book stages. Times are steady walking pace, calibrated against the guidebook stage times, with no stops - add lunch, photos and faffing on top.
